Tsunami hazard and riskÂ¶

Tsunamis are rare events that are often accompanied by advanced warnings. It is not possible to predict where and when the next tsunami might occur. However, tsunami warning centers know which events could likely generate tsunamis and issue warnings. This is done through networks of ocean and coastal sea-level observation systems designed to detect tsunamis. These early-warning systems facilitate evacuation to areas of high-ground outside of the tsunami inundation regions.

Often when tsunamis are triggered by distant events, that allows for longer evacuation times. Thus, the coastal communities can be evacuated. When the events are triggered by local events, there might not be sufficient time for a horizontal evacuation. In such scenarios, a potential solution is vertical evacuation above the rising waters into high-rise buildings and other structures with strength and resilience necessary to resist the effects of tsunami waves. Tsunami risk assessment and management can include four major stages:

  • Survey: In the first stage, a survey of existing structural heights is conducted. Further on based on historical records and preliminary simulations, the necessary heights are obtained for the existing structures.

  • Off-shore simulation: Here, the possible cases of tsunamis and storm surge are simulations and the resulting wave heights are studied.

  • Inland simulation: Here the resulting flood is simulated to calculate the possible and projected flood depths, velocity, time-series of flood etc.

  • Time requirement: Finally, the time needed for evacuation and possible loss of life, in the event of a tsunami are calculated.

Hazard vs. riskÂ¶

Hazard is generally defined as the potential for an event to occur while risk is related to the consequences, given the occurence of an event. Hazard is often characterized in terms of the potential magnitude of the site-specific effects like inundation, height of runup, flow depth / velocity. Risk is measured in terms of damage, loss of function, injury and loss of life. The risk depends on a multitude of factors including vulnerability of the built environment, population density, warning times, ability to evacuate etc.
